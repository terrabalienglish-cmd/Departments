Volunteers
Operations

Volunteers are not unpaid staff. They are the most invested layer of the Terra community — founders, creatives, and operators who give their time because they believe in what Terra is building. This department exists to make sure that investment is returned: through real skills, real friendships, real growth. Volunteers who feel like they're growing stay. Volunteers who feel like they're serving leave. This manual builds the first kind of experience.

The Problem to Solve

Why Volunteers Leave

Before building the systems, understand the disease. Volunteer churn is almost never about time. It's about feeling.

Reason 01 — They feel used
They show up, do tasks, go home. Nobody asks them what they're learning or what they want to grow in. They feel like labor, not people. The fix: every volunteer has a growth goal and a person who checks in on it.
Reason 02 — They feel isolated
They only know the 2 people in their department. Terra never becomes their community — just their task. The fix: co-working days and cross-department collaboration that build real friendships across the team.
Reason 03 — They can't see the point
They don't see how their tasks connect to Terra's bigger picture. Attendance sheets feel pointless when you don't know what they feed into. The fix: regular visibility into the impact of what they're doing. Make the connection explicit and often.
The Fundamental Design Principle
Every structure in this department — co-working days, growth ladders, fun days, cross-collaboration — exists to answer one question for every volunteer: "Am I growing here, and do I belong here?" If the answer to both is yes, they stay. If either is no, they eventually leave — regardless of how much they like Terra's mission.
Section 01

Volunteer Roles

Six volunteer roles. Each serves one or more departments and builds one or more real skills. Volunteers are matched to roles based on what they want to develop — not just what Terra needs. Both matter. If only Terra's needs are served, volunteers leave.

Events Crew
Serves: Workshops · Mentorship · Logistics
Set up, registration desk, attendee experience, breakdown. Arrives 60 minutes early. Stays until venue is reset. The face of Terra at the door.
Grows in: event production, people skills, operational execution, calm under pressure
Content Crew
Serves: Marketing · Workshops · School
Photography, video, short-form content capture at events. Provides raw material to Marketing Lead within 24 hours. No posting without approval.
Grows in: visual storytelling, content strategy, brand voice, creative eye
Compliance Crew
Serves: Compliance · All departments
Attendance tracking at workshops and mentorship sessions. Data submitted within 2 hours of each event. Accuracy is the job. No shortcuts.
Grows in: data discipline, systems thinking, operational consistency, attention to detail
Community Host
Serves: Workshops · School · Mentorship
Circulates at networking sessions. Connects attendees to each other. Warm, curious, represents Terra's values in every interaction. Captures new names missed at registration.
Grows in: networking, community building, conversation skills, relationship management
Admin & Comms Crew
Serves: Mentorship · School · Sponsorship
Supports department leads with written comms, Notion updates, follow-up messages, and scheduling. Works inside established templates — does not go off-script. Trains directly under department leads.
Grows in: professional writing, systems management, operations, business administration
School Support
Serves: School
Assists during School sessions: attends sessions, supports students with exercise facilitation, submits attendance to Compliance, follows up on assignment submission reminders. Works directly with School Director.
Grows in: teaching, facilitation, curriculum understanding, educational systems
The Matching Principle
When a volunteer applies, the Volunteer Lead asks: what do you actually want to get better at over the next 6 months? The role assignment follows that answer. A founder who wants to get better at content goes to Content Crew — even if Events Crew is more urgent. Terra gets more from a motivated volunteer in the right role than an unwilling one in the wrong one.
Section 02

Recruitment & Onboarding

The best volunteer candidates are already in the Terra ecosystem — workshop attendees, School graduates, mentorship alumni. The Volunteer Lead watches for them. The process brings them in properly.

Process — Volunteer Recruitment
01
Identify candidates from within the community
The best volunteer candidates show themselves before they're asked. They're the people who help set up without being told, who introduce people to each other at workshops, who engage deeply in School sessions. Volunteer Lead watches every event for these people. They are flagged in Notion with a note: "potential volunteer — observed [behaviour] at [event]."
Trigger: Ongoing. At every Terra event.
Volunteer LeadNotion pipeline
02
Personal invitation — never a public call
Volunteers are not recruited through a general "we need volunteers" post. They are personally invited. "I noticed how you handled the registration desk last week — have you thought about getting more involved in how Terra runs?" A personal invitation signals that they are specifically wanted, not generally needed. This distinction determines the quality of response.
Trigger: Candidate identified and flagged
Volunteer LeadWhatsApp direct or in-person
03
Interest conversation — what do you want to grow in?
20-minute conversation. Not an interview. Three questions: What are you working on right now? What do you want to get better at in the next 6 months? How much time can you genuinely commit per month? These answers determine role fit. There are no wrong answers — only mismatched roles. Document the conversation in Notion.
Trigger: Candidate expresses interest
Volunteer LeadNotion — volunteer record
04
Role assignment and volunteer agreement
Based on the conversation, Volunteer Lead proposes a role. Explains exactly what the role involves, which department they'll work with, what they'll learn, and what the time commitment is. Volunteer signs the volunteer agreement — not a legal contract, a mutual commitment document. It protects both sides.
Trigger: Role agreed
Volunteer LeadGoogle Drive (signed agreement)
05
Welcome to the volunteer team — proper onboarding session
60-minute onboarding with Volunteer Lead and the department lead they'll be working with. Covers: Terra's values, how the volunteer team works, their specific role in detail, the co-working day schedule, WhatsApp groups they're joining, who their buddy is (existing volunteer who mentors them for first 4 weeks). They leave with everything they need to show up to their first event.
Trigger: Agreement signed
Volunteer Lead + Department LeadIn-person or online
06
Buddy assigned — first 4 weeks of shadowing
Every new volunteer is paired with an existing volunteer from the same or adjacent role for their first 4 weeks. The buddy's job: answer questions, make introductions, include them in the social layer of the team. Not training — belonging. The buddy relationship is the single most important thing in the first month.

Volunteer Lead assigns buddyWhatsApp introduction

30-day check-in from Volunteer Lead
One month after joining, Volunteer Lead checks in 1:1. How's the role feeling? Is the growth goal being met? Anything they want to change? This is the earliest intervention point. Most volunteers who leave do so silently — this check-in catches the problem before it becomes a departure.

Section 03

Terra Co-Working Days

Monthly. Every volunteer, every department, in one place. Not a meeting. Not a training. A structured day of focused work, shared learning, and cross-department connection — followed by food and genuine time together. This is where the community is built.

The Design Intention
Terra's Co-Working Day is inspired by structured productivity sessions but adapted for a community team — not a company. The goal is not maximum output. The goal is that after 5 hours together, every volunteer has done real work, learned something from someone outside their department, and feels closer to the people around them. That combination — usefulness plus belonging — is what keeps people showing up.
Full Co-Working Day Schedule (5.5 Hours + Social)
9:30am
Arrival & Setup
Volunteers arrive, get settled, coffee and food available. Volunteer Lead does a brief room welcome — sets the tone, not a long speech. Everyone writes their personal focus for the day on a sticky note or shared board: "Today I'm working on [X]."
Social
10:00am
Focused Work Block 1 — Individual Priority Task (90 min)
Everyone works on their single most important Terra task. No meetings. No group discussions. Heads down. Events Crew might be building their next event checklist. Content Crew editing reels. Admin Crew updating Notion. Each person works on what only they can advance today.
Focused Work
11:30am
Active Break (15 min)
Everyone stands up. Volunteer Lead leads 5 minutes of light movement — stretching, a short walk around the venue, a breathing reset. Then 10 minutes of open conversation. No devices. Get water. Talk to someone from a different department.
Energy Reset
11:45am
Cross-Department Skill Share (45 min)
The heart of the day. One volunteer from one department teaches or shares something with the whole group. 20 minutes presentation, 25 minutes open discussion. Topics rotate monthly across departments. Examples: Content Crew teaches "how to film a 60-second reel that converts." Compliance Crew shares "how to read attendance data to spot patterns." Events Crew shares "the 3 things that make a registration desk work." The presenter chooses their own topic — it must be something they've actually learned from doing.
Cross-Department Learning
12:30pm
Lunch (45 min)
Proper lunch. Volunteer Lead organises food — either catered simply or a local warung near the venue. Seated together. No phones at the table. This is not a break from the day — it is part of the day. Relationships are built at meals, not in meetings.
Community
1:15pm
Focused Work Block 2 — Cross-Department Collaboration (60 min)
Structured cross-department pairing. Volunteer Lead assigns pairs or trios from different roles to work on a shared challenge for 60 minutes. See the Cross-Collaboration Protocols section for how pairings work. Each pair has a defined output — not "discuss" but "produce." A draft, a plan, a list, a decision. Real work, across departments, together.
Cross-Collaboration
2:15pm
Active Break (10 min)
Short reset. Walk, stretch, water. Volunteer Lead uses this moment to informally check in with anyone who seems disengaged or low energy.
Energy Reset
2:25pm
Focused Work Block 3 — Finish What You Started (60 min)
Final work block. Each person returns to their individual priority task and aims to reach a "done for today" state. The rule: by the end of this block, write down what you completed — not what you worked on. Completions are what go on the Wins Board.
Focused Work
3:25pm
Wins Round & Close (20 min)
Everyone shares one thing they completed today — on the Wins Board (whiteboard, sticky notes, or shared Notion page). No minimising. "I finished editing that reel" counts. "I sorted out the registration sheet" counts. Then one thing they learned from someone else today. Volunteer Lead closes with a brief look ahead — what's coming up in Terra's world that they should know about.
Reflection
3:45pm
Social Time — Optional but Encouraged (open ended)
The formal day is done. But the venue stays open and the energy stays warm. Drinks, food, or a nearby spot. Nobody is required to stay — but most do, because this is where the conversations happen that make people feel like they belong. Volunteer Lead stays for at least 1 hour.
Community Time
Co-Working Day Logistics
  • Frequency
    Once per month. Same week each month (e.g. second Saturday). Consistency builds anticipation.
  • Venue
    Co-working space, café with a private room, or a Terra sponsor venue. Reliable WiFi. Power sockets. Capacity for 10–20. Booked at least 2 weeks ahead by Logistics.
  • Who attends
    All active volunteers. Department leads are invited but not required — their presence is a gift, not a given.
  • Skill Share Rotation
    Monthly skill share presenter scheduled 2 months ahead. Every volunteer presents at least once per year. Volunteer Lead coordinates the schedule.
  • Wins Board
    Physical whiteboard or Notion shared page. Wins stay visible. At the next co-working day, the previous month's wins are briefly celebrated before the new day begins.
What Makes it Work vs What Kills It
  • Works: Consistent date and time
    Volunteers plan their lives around it. Moving it breaks the habit.
  • Works: Real food at lunch
    A proper shared meal is worth more than any team-building exercise.
  • Works: Skill share is volunteer-led, not leader-led
    Peers teaching peers builds respect and confidence simultaneously.
  • Kills it: Making it feel like a meeting
    If the agenda is just Terra updates, nobody will want to come.
  • Kills it: No social time at the end
    The relationships form in unstructured time. Cutting it cuts the point.
  • Kills it: Low-energy facilitation
    The Volunteer Lead sets the energy ceiling for the room. They must be on.
Section 04

Cross-Collaboration Protocols

The 60-minute collaboration block during co-working days pairs volunteers from different departments on a shared challenge. These pairings are not random — they are deliberately matched to create knowledge transfer between roles that would not naturally interact. Here are the standard pairings and their collaboration briefs.

Content Crew
↔
Events Crew
Challenge: "How do we capture better content at events without disrupting the event?"
Content Crew explains what shots and moments they need. Events Crew explains what's happening on the ground and when. Together they produce: a co-working day output brief — which moments at the next event are capturable, from where, without being intrusive. One A4 page, done in the session.
Compliance Crew
↔
Community Host
Challenge: "How do we capture every attendee without making registration feel like a checkpoint?"
Compliance needs the data. Community Host knows how to make human interaction warm. Together they review the current registration process and redesign one moment in it — the greeting, the handoff, the way the form is presented — to feel like welcome, not admin.
Admin & Comms Crew
↔
School Support
Challenge: "How do we make student communication feel personal at scale?"
Admin Crew owns the templates. School Support is in the room with students and knows how they actually communicate. Together they review one email template currently being used and rewrite it to sound like a person, not a system. The rewrite is the output — submitted to School Director after the session.
Content Crew
↔
Community Host
Challenge: "What are the moments at Terra events worth documenting — and why?"
Community Host is in the room seeing what moves people. Content Crew decides what to film. Together they build: a list of the top 5 moments worth capturing at a Terra workshop and why each one matters for the story Terra is telling. This becomes a reference document for Content Crew at all future events.
Events Crew
↔
Admin & Comms Crew
Challenge: "How do we brief Logistics better so events run smoother?"
Events Crew knows what breaks on the day. Admin Crew knows how to structure a brief clearly. Together they review the current event brief template and identify the 3 things that are always missing or unclear. Then they add them. The updated template is submitted to Logistics Lead.
School Support
↔
Compliance Crew
Challenge: "How do we make attendance data useful, not just accurate?"
Compliance has the data. School Support sees how it affects the classroom. Together they look at last cohort's attendance patterns and identify: what does the data reveal about when students disengage? The output is a one-paragraph observation submitted to the School Director — turning raw numbers into insight.
How Pairings Are Chosen Each Month
Volunteer Lead selects 2–3 pairings for each co-working day based on: what challenges the departments are currently facing, which volunteers haven't worked together yet, and which knowledge transfers would create the most value right now. Pairings rotate so that over 6 months, every volunteer has worked directly with every other department at least once. This is not accidental — it's deliberate architecture.

Q1 Team Building Day
Team Building Days — Quarterly

Once per quarter. A half-day structured experience followed by an unstructured afternoon. The morning builds trust through a shared challenge. The afternoon builds friendship through shared time. Both are necessary.

Q1 — Skill Exchange Workshop
Morning: Every volunteer teaches the group one skill from their own life — not their Terra role. A volunteer who surfs teaches a paddling technique. One who codes shares a shortcut. One who cooks makes one dish. Completely non-work. It reveals who people are beyond their role.
Why it works: When you know someone as a whole person, you're more committed to them. Churn is personal — the fix is personal.
Q2 — Challenge Day
Morning: Mixed-department teams tackle a real Terra problem together. Example: "Design the ideal volunteer onboarding experience." Each team presents to the Volunteer Lead and COO. Best idea gets implemented. Real stakes, real output, cross-department collaboration under light pressure.
Why it works: Volunteers feel heard. Their ideas shape Terra. That ownership is a powerful retention mechanism.
Q3 — Bali Exploration Day
Full day. The team goes somewhere together — a different part of Bali none of them have been to, or an experience outside their usual social range. A rice field walk in Jatiluwih. A cooking class in Ubud. A sunrise at Batur. The activity is secondary to the day spent in each other's company.
Why it works: Shared unusual experiences create disproportionate bonds. They'll talk about this day for months.
Fun Days — Every 6 Weeks

No agenda. No outputs. No Notion updates. A group of people doing something enjoyable in Bali together. The Volunteer Lead organises it. The only rule: it must be something that only makes sense in Bali, and it must be easy to say yes to.

Active
Sunrise Surf Session
Rent boards, hire a local instructor for beginners. Canggu beach at 6am. Breakfast at a warung after. Back before 9am. Easy yes, low cost, physical endorphins, great photos.
Why: Physical shared experience + beautiful Bali setting = emotional memory that outlasts any team building exercise.
Social
Communal Cook & Eat
Someone's villa or a rented kitchen space. Everyone brings one ingredient. Volunteer Lead sets a loose theme. Cook together, eat together, clean together. No phones during the meal.
Why: Cooking and eating together is the oldest form of community building. It works every time.
Cultural
Temple Visit & Local Guide
One of Bali's lesser-visited temples. A local who can explain what they're seeing. Lunch at a nearby local place. Half day. Respectful, interesting, memorable.
Why: Shared cultural curiosity is a bonding mechanism. It signals that the team values depth over entertainment.
Playful
Bali Movie Night
Outdoor or rooftop projector. Someone's place or a venue. One film chosen by vote in advance. Snacks, drinks, blankets. Optional: short "why I chose this film" from whoever suggested it.
Why: Low effort for the group, high intimacy. People share what they love. That's the point.
Active
Volleyball or Badminton
Beach volleyball at Seminyak or a badminton court in Denpasar. Mixed teams, casual rules. Competitive enough to be fun, relaxed enough for everyone. Food and drinks after.
Why: Physical play removes hierarchies. Nobody cares who's the department lead when the ball is in the air.
Adventurous
Day Trip to the Mountains
Munduk or Bedugul. Rent a van together. Waterfalls, warungs, views. Full day out of the usual Canggu-Seminyak orbit. Returns everyone with the same shared memory.
Why: Leaving the routine together is a bonding accelerant. The van ride alone builds more connection than most structured activities.
Section 06

Volunteer Growth Ladder

Volunteers need to see a path, not just a role. The growth ladder shows what's possible — from first month to department leadership. Progress is recognised, not assumed. The Volunteer Lead checks in on each person's position every quarter.

What They Do
What They Develop
New Volunteer
Months 1–2
Shadows a buddy. Supports at events. Learns the role. Attends first co-working day. Does not own anything yet — observes and assists.
Orientation, confidence, relationships. Understands how Terra works as a system, not just as a series of tasks.
Active Volunteer
Months 2–6
Fully executing their role independently. Attends co-working days. Does their first skill share presentation. Works cross-department in collaboration blocks.
Technical skills of their role. Cross-department understanding. Presenting and sharing knowledge. Reliability and consistency.
Senior Volunteer
Months 6–12
Mentors new volunteers as a buddy. Contributes to how the role is structured — not just executes it. Proposes improvements to processes they see breaking. Involved in co-working day design.
Leadership, mentoring, systems thinking. Their growth goal shifts from "I want to learn X" to "I want to improve how X works for everyone."
Role Lead
Month 12+
Owns their volunteer role. Coordinates others in that role. Briefs their department's volunteer crew before events. Reports directly to department lead and Volunteer Lead. Has their own 1:1 with Volunteer Lead monthly.
Management, coordination, accountability. Effectively operating as a junior department lead. Terra is investing in their development at a meaningful level.
Quarterly Growth Review — Process
01
Volunteer self-assessment form sent 1 week before review
Short Google Form. Questions: What am I most proud of this quarter? What is one skill I've developed? What am I still struggling with? What do I want to focus on next quarter? Am I in the right role for where I want to go? This is their preparation — the conversation doesn't start from scratch.
Volunteer Lead sendsGoogle Form
02
20-minute 1:1 with Volunteer Lead
Not a performance review. A growth conversation. Volunteer Lead reads the self-assessment before the meeting. Conversation covers: what's working, what's not, what the next 90 days look like, and whether their ladder position should change. If a volunteer is ready to progress, it is named and acknowledged — not assumed.
Volunteer Lead + VolunteerNotion — volunteer record updated
03
Exit conversation — if a volunteer leaves
If a volunteer decides to step back, Volunteer Lead has a short, kind conversation: what are they moving on to, what would have made them stay longer, is there a lighter-touch way to stay involved (occasional event support rather than full commitment)? Their exit is logged in Notion with the primary reason. Patterns across exits drive system changes — not individual blame.

Section 09

Operating Rules

These protect the quality of the volunteer experience. Every rule exists to prevent a specific form of churn. Know what each one is protecting against.

Non-Negotiable Operating Rules — Volunteers Department
01Volunteers are never recruited through a public "we need help" call. They are personally invited. The personal invitation is what signals that they are specifically wanted — not generally needed. This distinction determines the quality of who joins.
02Every volunteer is matched to a role based on their growth goal, not just Terra's needs. Both must be served. A volunteer in the wrong role for their growth will leave within 3 months regardless of mission alignment.
03Every new volunteer has a buddy for their first 4 weeks. The buddy's job is belonging, not training. The difference matters.
04The 30-day check-in happens. No exceptions. Most volunteers who leave do so silently — this check-in is the earliest intervention point.
05Co-working days are monthly, consistent, and always followed by social time. Moving the date breaks the habit. Cutting the social time cuts the point.
06The skill share at co-working days is always volunteer-led. Never Volunteer Lead or department lead as presenter. Peers teaching peers is more powerful and more respectful of volunteer capability.
07Cross-department pairings in collaboration blocks produce a tangible output — a document, a rewrite, a list, a decision. "We had a good chat" is not an output. Real work builds real respect between departments.
08Fun days have no agenda and no outputs. The moment a fun day becomes an opportunity for a Terra announcement or a team debrief, it stops being a fun day. Protect the category.
09Quarterly growth reviews happen for every active volunteer. Ladder progression is named explicitly — not assumed. Recognition that is felt is more powerful than recognition that exists on paper.
10Every volunteer who leaves has an exit conversation. Their reason is logged in Notion. When 3 volunteers leave for the same reason, it is a systems problem — not a people problem. Fix the system.
11The volunteer WhatsApp group is maintained as a social channel, not just an operational one. Volunteer Lead actively drives warmth and recognition in it. A quiet WhatsApp group is an early warning sign.
12Volunteers are publicly recognised at Terra events when they do something exceptional. Not constantly — specifically. Specific recognition has ten times the impact of general appreciation. The department lead and Volunteer Lead coordinate on who to name and for what.
"The community you build internally is the community you project externally."
